The Myth of Sisyphus

Philosophical Essay by Albert Camus
Original titleLe Mythe de Sisyphe
Published1942
SubjectAbsurdism
Key ThemePhilosophical Suicide

The Myth of Sisyphus is a 1942 philosophical essay by Albert Camus. In the essay, Camus introduces his philosophy of the Absurd: the futile search for meaning, unity, and clarity in the face of an unintelligible world devoid of God and eternal truths or values.

The Myth

Camus uses the Greek myth of Sisyphus as a metaphor for the human condition. Sisyphus is condemned by the gods to roll a giant boulder up a hill, only for it to roll back down as soon as he reaches the top—a cycle he must repeat for eternity.

"The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
Philosophical Rebellion

According to Camus, once we recognize that life has no inherent meaning, we have two choices: despair or rebellion. Camus argues that we should embrace the struggle. By continuing to roll our "boulder" despite knowing the task is futile, we become masters of our own fate.

Why I Value This Essay

This work serves as a powerful reminder that happiness is not found in the "meaning" of the destination, but in the act of the journey itself. It teaches me to find strength in persistence and to create my own joy even when the world feels silent or indifferent.
